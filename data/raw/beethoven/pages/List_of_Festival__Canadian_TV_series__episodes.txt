The following is an episode list of the Canadian CBC Television anthology series, Festival.
The series premiered on  10 October 1960 (1960-10-10), with the episode, "H.M.S. Pinafore", and aired for nine seasons until 26 March 1969 (1969-03-26). Episode duration typically ranged from one hour to ninety minutes. A few episodes in seasons five and eight, and several episodes in seasons six and seven, were as short as thirty minutes. Special episodes ranged from two to three hours in duration.


== Series overview ==


== Episodes ==
Festival was occasionally pre-empted (e.g., the week of 10 April 1961) with other programming, such as specials from the Omnibus or Hallmark Hall of Fame series, or by sports (NHL hockey, football), or by other special programs. These are noted above the episode tables that follow.


=== Season 1 (1960–61) ===

Notes:

For four weeks, the Montreal 9.30 p.m. time-slot was listed as "Festival '61–Omnibus" replacing CBC productions with programming by the American educational variety series, Omnibus (hosted by Alistair Cooke, featuring plays, music performances, and discussions). The Omnibus episode titles and their Festival air dates follow: "Night People" (5 December 1960), "Abraham Lincoln: The Early Years" (30 January 1961), "Fierce, Funny and Far Out" (27 February 1961), and "An Omnibus of American Song" (20 March 1961).
For three other weeks, other programs aired; The Bell Telephone Hour on 28 November 1960, the Hallmark Hall of Fame Christmas opera The Golden Child on 12 December 1960, and NHL Hockey on 10 April 1961.
For the week of 17 April 1961 (1961-04-17), "The Subject is Beethoven" was re-aired. It originally aired the week of 6 February 1961.


=== Season 2 (1961–62) ===

Notes:

Beginning this season, the program was no longer billed as Festival '61, but simply as Festival.
Most of the weeks in which Festival did not air, programming was pre-empted by special episodes of Camera Canada.
"The Offshore Island" which aired the week of 12 March 1962, re-aired later in the season on 14 May 1962 (1962-05-14).
The week of 2 April 1962 (1962-04-02) was a repeat of "The Luck of Ginger Coffey" that originally aired the week of 19 June 1961 in season one.


=== Season 3 (1962–63) ===

Notes:

Weeks not indicated were pre-empted by special broadcasts such as Camera Canada or The Telephone Hour.
National election coverage pre-empted Festival on 8 April 1963.
"A Cradle of Willow" that aired the week of 24 December 1962 (1962-12-24) is a repeat from season one, the week of 25 December 1961.
"A Book with Chapters in It" aired twice this season, 8 October 1962 (1962-10-08) and 29 April 1963 (1963-04-29).
"David, Chapter II" aired twice this season, 28 January 1963 (1963-01-28) and 20 May 1963 (1963-05-20).
"Othello" ran 2 hours 15 minutes, compared to the usual 60-90 minutes. Therefore it was originally broadcast at 8:30 EST instead of 9:30.
"Venus Observed" was originally scheduled for broadcast 7 January 1963, but delayed due to videotape erasure.


=== Season 4 (1963–64) ===

Notes:

Weeks not indicated were pre-empted by special broadcasts such as Camera Canada, Horizon or Intertel.
In some but not all markets, NHL hockey playoffs pre-empted the Festival episode "Twelfth Night" on 8 April 1964.
"Pale Horse, Pale Rider" aired twice this season; 23 October 1963 (1963-10-23), and 18 March 1964 (1964-03-18).
"Diary of a Scoundrel" aired twice this season; 25 December 1963 (1963-12-25), and 1 April 1964 (1964-04-01).
The week of 15 April 1964 (1964-04-15), Festival was broadcast as a Special Edition at 8:30 PM EST to accommodate the 3-hour (170 minute) debut of the BBC TV / Danmarks Radio co-production of Hamlet at Elsinore to commemorate Shakespeare's 400th birthday.
"Othello" re-aired this season on 22 April 1964 (1964-04-22) in honour of Shakespeare's 400th birthday. It originally aired in season three on 22 April 1963 (1963-04-22). Its duration of 2 hours and 15 minutes is substantially longer than other episodes (60-90 minutes).


=== Season 5 (1964–65) ===

Notes:

Three episodes from season four reaired this season; "Roots" on 21 October 1964 (1964-10-21), "A Very Close Family" on 24 February 1965 (1965-02-24), and "Pale Horse, Pale Rider" on 17 March 1965 (1965-03-17).
Weeks pre-empted by other programming, including; Intertel on 23 December 1964 (1964-12-23), Hallmark Hall of Fame on 27 January 1965 (1965-01-27), NHL hockey on 10 March 1965 (1965-03-10),  Camera Canada on 16 June 1965 (1965-06-16), and a special on China called The 700 Million on 4 November 1964 (1964-11-04) and its repeat airing on 30 December 1964 (1964-12-30).
The Festival time-slot was also filled for special productions:
On 13 January 1965 (1965-01-13), Festival aired the National Film Board of Canada one-hour documentary Bethune produced by Donald Brittain and John Kemeny, which had debuted earlier in November 1964 at the Leipzig International Festival in East Germany.
On 17 February 1965 (1965-02-17), the Cariboo Country episode "The Education of Phyllistine" aired  on Festival after its screening at the Montreal International Film Festival (August 7th-13th 1964). The one-hour episode was edited together from two half-hour shows, and won a Canadian Film Award.
On 14 April 1965 (1965-04-14), Claude Jutra's 1963 film À tout prendre aired on Festival.


=== Season 6 (1965–66) ===

Notes:

Three episodes were season five reruns; "Let Me Count the Ways" on 10 November 1965 (1965-11-10), "Ricter and Forrester - In Praise of Great Performers" on 22 June 1966 (1966-06-22), and "Music In Camera - In Praise of Great Performers" on 29 June 1966 (1966-06-29).
Two season six episodes aired twice; "Romeo and Juliet" on 2 March 1966 (1966-03-02), and "Heritage" on 9 February 1966 (1966-02-09).
Other weeks were pre-empted by other programming; pro football on 17 November 1965 (1965-11-17); an NFB documentary, "Buy Low, Sell High" on 27 April 1966 (1966-04-27); the The Bob Hope Vietnam Christmas Show and an episode of Cariboo Country called "How to Break a Quarterhorse" on 19 January 1966 (1966-01-19); a re-broadcast of the NFB documentary Bethune on 9 February 1966 (1966-02-09); and on 16 March 1966 (1966-03-16), the NFB documentary Memorandum (1965) with the 1963 short film La contrebasse ("Contre Basse") directed by Maurice Fasquel, which he adapted from Anton Chekhov's short story Roman s kontrabasom. Fasquel's film won an award at the San Sebastián International Film Festival.
Festival also had short presentations that followed programing by other corporations and producers:
The week of 8 December 1965 (1965-12-08) the Festival time-slot was used to air the 75-minute BBC film Culloden originally broadcast on 14 December 1964. The remaining 15 minutes of air-time (totaling 90 minutes) was filled with a song recital by Montreal singer Monique Leyrac who had won the top award at an international song festival in Sopot, Poland that summer. These two segments were rerun the week of 13 April 1966 (1966-04-13).
The week of 16 February 1966 (1966-02-16) the first hour of Festival aired the NFB 44-minute documentary Ladies and Gentlemen... Mr. Leonard Cohen produced by John Kemeny, followed by the half-hour Festival episode, "The Scotch".


=== Season 7 (1966–67) ===

Notes:

Festival reruns include "Contre Basse" on 3 May 1967 (1967-05-03), "Silent Night, Lonely Night" on 14 December 1966 (1966-12-14) "No Two People" on 21 December 1966 (1966-12-21), "Heloise and Abelard" on 2 November 1966 (1966-11-02), "Spirit of the Deed" on 18 January 1967 (1967-01-18), and "Fifteen Miles of Broken Glass" on 22 February 1967 (1967-02-22).
For three weeks Music Canada aired in Festival's time-slot, on 7 December 1966 (1966-12-07), 4 January 1967 (1967-01-04), and 12 April 1967 (1967-04-12).
Other programming that pre-empted Festival include the Hallmark Hall of Fame specials, "Blithe Spirit" on 23 November 1966 (1966-11-23), and "Anastasia" on 1 February 1967 (1967-02-01), Intertel on 7 December 1966 and 14 December 1966 (1966-12-14), and Bob Hope on 18 January 1967 (1967-01-18).
Special programming that pre-empted Festival includes a News Special re-broadcast of the funeral procession of late Governor General of Canada, Georges Vanier on 8 March 1967 (1967-03-08), the Centennial Performance on 19 April 1967 (1967-04-19), and the NFB documentary The Things I Cannot Change on 3 May 1967 (1967-05-03).
For the week of 16 November 1966 (1966-11-16), Festival aired the 90-minute documentary The Golden Ring (1965), a United Kingdom BBC production released on 16 May 1965, about the making of the Götterdämmerung ("Twilight of the Gods") recording, the last of the four epic music dramas from Richard Wagner's cycle Der Ring des Nibelungen ("The Ring of the Nibelung").
For the week of 21 December 1966 (1966-12-21), Festival commissioned the drama special "How to Break a Quarter Horse" for the upcoming 1967 Canadian Centennial. The episode was developed from the anthology series Cariboo Country.
The week of 5 April 1967 (1967-04-05), Festival aired the BBC production of Cathy Come Home which originally aired on The Wednesday Play on 16 November 1966.


=== Season 8 (1967–68) ===

Notes:

Two Centennial Performance episodes pre-empted new Festival episodes on 4 October 1967 (1967-10-04) and 15 November 1967 (1967-11-15), and the NFB Centennial film The Ernie Game aired on 8 November 1967 (1967-11-08).
Other programs that pre-empted Festival include, pro football on 22 November 1967 (1967-11-22), The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1968) on 3 January 1968 (1968-01-03), The Music Man (1962) on 31 January 1968 (1968-01-31), the BBC film "The Matador" on 7 February 1968 (1968-02-07), the BBC documentary Soldiers of the Widow (1967) on 21 February 1968 (1968-02-21), Mission: Impossible on 20 March 1968 (1968-03-20), the 1967 Quentin Durgens, M.P. episode "The Road to Chaldaea" on 27 March 1968 (1968-03-27), Intertel on 17 April 1968 (1968-04-17), and Stanley Cup hockey on 1 May 1968 (1968-05-01).
Special programs that pre-empted Festival include, Film Makers and With Love, Sophia on 25 October 1967 (1967-10-25), the 60-minute documentary The Secret Years of Eldorado about Canadian participation in the nuclear age on 10 January 1968 (1968-01-10), the Newsmagazine episode "The Secrets War" on 6 March 1968 (1968-03-06), live coverage of the Liberal Party Convention on 3 April 1968 (1968-04-03), and live coverage of the Oscar Awards on 10 April 1968 (1968-04-10).
The week of 24 January 1968 (1968-01-24), the American Hallmark Hall of Fame television film Elizabeth the Queen (1968) aired on Festival starring Judith Anderson, and Charlton Heston.
For the week of 24 January 1968 (1968-01-24), Festival had procured and aired Giuseppe Verdi: Messa da Requiem (1967). Directed by French film-maker Henri-Georges Clouzot, it is a performance of Verdi's Requiem Mass with Herbert von Karajan conducting the Chorus and Orchestra of La Scala, Milan. The soloists are soprano Leontyne Price, mezzo-soprano Fiorenza Cossotto, tenor Luciano Pavarotti, and basso Nicolai Ghiaurov.


=== Season 9 (1968–69) ===

Notes:

"Traveller Without Luggage" from season two was rerun on 26 February 1969 (1969-02-26).
Programs which pre-empted Festival include Bob Hope and Public Eye on 27 November 1968 (1968-11-27), and Christmas specials on 25 December 1968 (1968-12-25).
Special programming that aired in the Festival time-slot follows.
The 1966 BBC production of Benjamin Britten's opera Billy Budd, based on the 1891 novel by Herman Melville, aired on 20 November 1968 (1968-11-20) from 8:00 to 10:45 PM.
"Edmonton Contest" on 4 December 1968 (1968-12-04) was a CBC-TV music special hosted by Brian Priestman, with five finalists of the National Performing Artists Competition, organized by the Edmonton Symphony Orchestra.
"Sir Ernest Laudamus" / "Enest MacMillan: A Musical Tribute" was a concert held on 20 November at Massey Hall with the Toronto Mendelssohn Choir, Festival Singers of Canada and Toronto Symphony Orchestra conducted by Elmer Iseler, and aired on 18 December 1968 (1968-12-18).
Public Eye aired at 9 PM (1 hour) and a Music Special (10 PM) featuring Canada's National Youth Orchestra, with musicians 14-24 years-old, presented its first television concert, with Franz-Paul Decker conducting 108 musicians, on 8 January 1969 (1969-01-08).
Three drama specials developed from the anthology series Cariboo Country, and aired on Festival, including Sister Balonika which was broadcast on 26 March 1969 (1969-03-26). It was written by Paul St. Pierre, directed by Philip Keatley and filmed on-location in Richmond, and at Hollyburn Film Studios, West Vancouver. It starred Vi Powlan in the lead role as Sister Veronica Anne, whom her students called "Bolanika." Cast included, Christine Green, Merv Campone, Nan Sandy, Susan Ringwood, Lloyd Berry, Jimmy Edwards, Ivor Harries, Diane Wassman, and Robert Clothier.
